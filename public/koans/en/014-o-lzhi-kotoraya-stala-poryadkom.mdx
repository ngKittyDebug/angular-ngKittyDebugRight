# Koan №14 — "On the Lie that Became Order"

<Source>Discovered in the sorting ledger of Nanzen-ji monastery, 1644 CE. The monks divided rice by weight. A novice applied an algorithm. The rice was distributed alphabetically. No one noticed for three months.</Source>

```js
[10, 9, 2, 21, 3].sort()
// [10, 2, 21, 3, 9]
```

<Student>"Master, I asked to sort numbers. It sorted strings."</Student>

<Master>"You asked 'sort.' You did not say — what exactly."</Master>

<Student>"But these are numbers!"</Student>

<Master>"For you — numbers. For .sort() without an argument — sequences of characters. It does what it knows, not what you meant."</Master>

<Student>"Then I must pass a comparator function?"</Student>

```js
[10, 9, 2, 21, 3].sort((a, b) => a - b)
// [2, 3, 9, 10, 21]
```

<Master>"Now you speak its language. You explained — what 'greater' means. Without explanation — it was guessing."</Master>

<Haiku>Numbers in a row. But whose order?</Haiku>

<Question>.sort() without an argument is one of the quietest betrayals in JavaScript. It does not break. It does not cry out. It simply does the wrong thing. If a function behaves predictably, but not as you expected — who was wrong: the function, or the one who did not read its nature?</Question>
