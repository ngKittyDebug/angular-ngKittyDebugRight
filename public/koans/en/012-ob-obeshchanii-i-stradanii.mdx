# Koan №12 — "On the Promise and the Suffering"

<Source>Recorded on a silk scroll found in the hands of the Kannon statue at Hase-dera temple, 1567 CE. The statue gripped it so tightly that the restorer broke her finger. He swore she did not want to let go.</Source>

```js
fetch('/api/truth')
  .then(r => r.json())
  .then(data => {
    throw new Error('doubt')
  })
// Unhandled Promise Rejection
```

<Student>"Master, I received the truth. I parsed it. Then I doubted. And the error... went into silence. No one heard it."</Student>

<Master>"Where does a cry go in an empty temple?"</Master>

<Student>"Nowhere... It fades."</Student>

<Master>"Unhandled Promise Rejection. Your doubt was not caught. It did not disappear — it festers. An error without .catch() is a wound without a bandage."</Master>

<Student>"Then I shall put .catch() on every promise!"</Student>

<Master>"And what will you write inside it?"</Master>

<Student>"console.log(err)..."</Student>

<Action>The master closed his eyes and was silent for a long time.</Action>

<Master>"To catch an error and write it in a log — that is not healing. That is a chronicle of suffering. You caught the pain. Acknowledged it. And left it lying there."</Master>

<Haiku>The wound is caught. Recorded. It continues to fester.</Haiku>

<Question>.catch(console.log) is the most common ritual in JavaScript. But if you catch an error and do nothing with it — is that better than not catching it at all? Is an empty handler mercy or cowardice?</Question>
