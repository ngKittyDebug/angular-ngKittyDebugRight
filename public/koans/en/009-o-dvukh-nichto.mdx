# Koan №9 — "On the Two Nothings"

<Source>From the correspondence of monks at Daitoku-ji monastery, 1456 CE. A junior monk asked a senior: "If emptiness is one — why does it have two names?" The senior burned the letter. This is a reconstruction.</Source>

```js
let a = undefined
let b = null

a == b    // true
a === b   // false
```

<Action>The student placed two stones before the master.</Action>

<Student>"Master, they are equal — but not identical. How?"</Student>

<Action>The master pointed to two empty chairs.</Action>

<Master>"In this chair no one sits, because no one came. In that — no one sits, because I removed the guest."</Master>

<Student>"Both are empty!"</Student>

<Master>"Both are empty. But the first never knew presence. The second — remembers absence."</Master>

<Student>"undefined is 'was never here.' null is 'was here, but was removed'?"</Student>

<Action>The master took both stones and clenched them in his fist.</Action>

<Master>"Ask the engine — they are similar. Ask the programmer — they are different. Ask the philosopher — they are both suffering."</Master>

<Haiku>Two empty footprints. One never came. One — has left.</Haiku>

<Question>undefined is assigned by the language itself — it is the language's silence. null is assigned by the programmer — it is their decision. But if both mean "nothing" — which is more honest? The one who is silent by accident, or the one who is silent on purpose?</Question>
